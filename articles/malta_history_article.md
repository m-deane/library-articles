# The Island That History Couldn't Leave Alone
### A holiday read on Malta — seven thousand years of everybody wanting a piece of it

---

There's a particular feeling you get when you arrive in Malta. It's not quite Europe, not quite Africa, and not quite the Middle East — and yet it's somehow all three at once. The limestone glows honey-gold in the afternoon light. The harbours are absurdly dramatic, all fortified walls and turrets stacked like theatre sets. The streets smell of diesel and jasmine and the sea. And everywhere — in the catacombs, under your feet, behind the scaffolding on a Valletta side street — there is the sense of something very, very old pressing up through the surface.

That's because Malta has been fought over, settled, colonised, bombed, besieged, sanctified and mythologised more times per square kilometre than almost anywhere else on earth. Sixty square miles of limestone rock sitting at the exact centre of the Mediterranean — and for seven thousand years, every great power in the western world has looked at it and thought: *I need that.*

---

## Before Anyone Had a Name for It

Let's start at the beginning, which in Malta's case is almost incomprehensibly far back.

Around 3600 BC, a people we know almost nothing about began building temples on this island. Not rough stone piles — temples. Proper, sophisticated megalithic structures with carved doorways, oval chambers, acoustic properties that modern architects would admire, and astronomical alignments suggesting a deep knowledge of the sky. The temples at Ħaġar Qim, Mnajdra, Tarxien, and the Ġgantija complex on nearby Gozo were already ancient when the first stone of Stonehenge was laid. They predate the Pyramids by centuries.

Archaeologists call these people the Temple Builders and, frustratingly, that's about all we can call them. They left no writing. Their language is gone. They carved squat, enigmatic figures — the famous "Fat Ladies" of the National Museum, ambiguous in gender, serene in expression — and they built an underground necropolis at Ħal Saflieni (the Hypogeum) that descends three storeys into the rock and is one of the most eerie and extraordinary spaces in the ancient world. They thrived for roughly fifteen hundred years, which is a remarkable run by any standard. Then, around 2350 BC, they were gone. Completely. Whether from drought, ecological collapse, disease or some internal catastrophe we don't understand, the Temple Builders simply ceased to exist, leaving their stones standing and their Sleeping Lady — a tiny terracotta figure found in the Hypogeum, curled in what looks like peaceful sleep — as perhaps the most quietly haunting object in all of Mediterranean archaeology.

The islands were eventually repopulated, Bronze Age people arrived, and life on Malta ground forward. But nothing quite matched that first flowering for another few thousand years.

## The Phoenicians and the Long Shadow of Carthage

Around 800 BC, the Phoenicians arrived. These were the great maritime traders of the ancient world, based in what is now Lebanon, and they were essentially the first people to connect the entire Mediterranean basin into a single commercial network. They called the island *Maleth* — "shelter" — and set up trading posts, introduced ironworking, and brought with them a Semitic language whose fingerprints remain in modern Maltese to this day. The colourful luzzu fishing boats that bob in Malta's harbours, eyes painted on the prow against the evil eye, are likely a Phoenician inheritance too.

When Phoenicia fell to Alexander the Great in 332 BC, Malta passed into the orbit of Carthage — the great Phoenician colony in what is now Tunisia, and Rome's most serious rival for dominance of the Mediterranean world. For Carthage, Malta wasn't just a port: it was a forward naval base, a textile producer, a strategic node. The Carthaginians held it for the better part of three centuries, and their influence ran deep. Punic religion, Punic language, Punic coins — the island absorbed it all.

Then came the Punic Wars, and everything changed.

---

## When the World Fought for the Mediterranean

The three Punic Wars between Rome and Carthage (264–146 BC) are one of those titanic historical conflicts that reshaped the entire western world — and Malta sat right in the middle of them, like a chip on the table that both sides had to have.

During the First Punic War, the island changed hands in brutal fighting. It fell back to Carthage, then was seized again during the Second Punic War in 218 BC, when the Roman consul Tiberius Sempronius Longus arrived and the Carthaginian garrison surrendered. Malta became Roman — and, crucially, became a *Foederata Civitas*: a federated city exempt from Roman tribute, a mark of privilege that spoke to how valuable the island was as an ally rather than a mere conquest.

What's remarkable is how slowly Rome actually Romanised Malta. The local coins kept Punic inscriptions long into the Roman period. The old Phoenician-derived language lingered. The culture was layers on layers — Punic underneath, Greek influence alongside, Roman on top — and all three coexisted without fully erasing each other. You can see this still in the surviving bilingual inscriptions and in the Cippi of Melqart, stone pillars found in Malta bearing text in both Phoenician and Greek. It was these very inscriptions that allowed an 18th-century French scholar, Jean-Jacques Barthélemy, to finally crack and decipher the extinct Phoenician alphabet — a Maltese stone unlocking a dead language, the way the Rosetta Stone unlocked Egyptian hieroglyphics.

## Romans, Roads, and a Very Famous Shipwreck

Under Roman rule, Malta flourished. Cities grew. Roads were built. Grand villas went up — the Domus Romana in what is now Rabat is a beautiful survivor, with Pompeian-style mosaics that feel genuinely startling in their quality. The island was important enough to rate a *municipium*, meaning its citizens had the rights of Roman citizens, a significant honour for a non-Italian territory.

But if you ask most Maltese people what defines the Roman era on their island, they'll tell you about the shipwreck.

In 60 AD, a ship carrying a Roman prisoner named Paul of Tarsus — Saint Paul, as history would have it — ran aground on the Maltese coast in a violent storm. Paul survived, spent three months on the island, allegedly healed the sick, survived a venomous snakebite that should have killed him, and converted the local population to Christianity. Whether you take the details literally or not, the cultural legacy is enormous. Malta has been proudly, tenaciously, sometimes fiercely Catholic ever since. Every town has a baroque church that looks two sizes too big for it. The feast days are extravagant civic spectacles. The faith runs through Maltese identity like a seam of rock. And it all — in myth and tradition — traces back to a storm-tossed Mediterranean crossing two thousand years ago.

## The Knights, the Great Siege, and the Making of a Fortress

The next chapter that shaped modern Malta most viscerally is the arrival of the Knights of St John in 1530. These were the Knights Hospitaller, a Catholic military order that had been kicked out of Rhodes by the Ottomans and were looking for a new home. The Holy Roman Emperor Charles V offered them Malta — "a few rocks," in the dismissive description of some contemporaries. The Knights accepted, somewhat grudgingly.

They built. And built. The fortifications they constructed around the Grand Harbour are among the most impressive military architecture in the world. Valletta, the capital they founded after the Great Siege of 1565, is an entire city that functions as a fortification.

The Siege of 1565 is the island's great epic moment. The Ottoman Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent sent an enormous fleet and army — perhaps 40,000 men — to finally crush the Knights and take Malta, opening the central Mediterranean to Ottoman domination. The Knights, with some Maltese fighters and Spanish reinforcements, held out through a summer of extraordinary violence. Fort St Elmo fell at horrifying cost. Defenders were crucified and floated across the harbour. The Knights retaliated in kind. By September, when a Spanish relief force finally landed, the Ottomans withdrew. Perhaps 2,500 defenders had held off an empire. Europe celebrated it as one of the great victories of the age. Malta had announced itself to the world.

## The Nurse of the Mediterranean

Fast forward to 1914, and Malta plays a completely different kind of heroic role.

When the First World War ground into stalemate on the Western Front and new fronts opened in the Dardanelles and Salonika, the casualties quickly outpaced anything the Allies had prepared for. The disastrous Gallipoli landings of April 1915 produced tens of thousands of wounded men who needed to go *somewhere* — and Malta, midway between the fighting and the safety of home, became the answer.

It happened fast. The first convoy of 600 patients arrived on 4 May 1915. More arrived the next day. By the end of May, over 4,000 Gallipoli casualties had reached the island. Barracks, schools, the Governor's Palace — anything with walls and a roof was converted. At its peak, Malta ran 27 hospitals and 25,000 beds, staffed by 334 medical officers and 913 nurses. A record 20,994 patients were treated in a single day. Over the course of the war, more than 136,000 sick and wounded soldiers were treated here — Australians, New Zealanders, Indians, Britons, and many others.

One contemporary writer captured it perfectly, writing that Malta "assumed the role of nurse, and her breakwaters seem like arms stretched out to receive her burden of suffering." The name stuck. Malta became the Nurse of the Mediterranean, and the letters ANZAC soldiers sent home — full of astonishment at Maltese warmth and hospitality, at chocolate distributed on the quayside, at wards decorated for Christmas — are some of the most touching documents of the entire war. The Pieta Military Cemetery in Valletta, where over 1,300 of those who didn't survive are buried, remains one of the most moving places on the island.

## The Most Bombed Place on Earth

If the First World War made Malta a place of healing, the Second made it a place of almost unimaginable endurance.

When Italy declared war on Britain in June 1940, Malta was in an extraordinarily exposed position. It lay just twenty minutes' flying time from Sicily. Its nearest Allied base was over a thousand miles away at Gibraltar. And it was one of the most strategically critical points on the map: Malta-based submarines and aircraft could reach Axis supply lines to Rommel's Afrika Korps, threatening to cut off the entire North African campaign. Both sides knew this. The Axis had to neutralise Malta. The Allies had to hold it at all costs.

What followed was the longest sustained aerial bombardment of World War Two. Over 3,340 air raids were registered. More than 15,000 tons of bombs fell on the islands. Over 10,000 buildings were destroyed. By 1942, the population of 270,000 was living largely underground, sheltering in tunnels cut from the same soft limestone their ancestors had used to build temples. Food rations fell to around 1,500 calories a day — the edge of starvation. Fuel was restricted to military use. There were points where anti-aircraft guns could fire only a few rounds per day to conserve ammunition.

In April 1942, King George VI did something unprecedented. He awarded the George Cross — Britain's highest civilian honour — not to a person, but to the entire island. His handwritten message read simply: *"To honour her brave people I award the George Cross to the Island Fortress of Malta, to bear witness to a heroism and devotion that will long be famous in history."* It was, and remains, one of only two collective awards of the George Cross ever made. The medal is still displayed at the National War Museum in Fort St Elmo, Valletta. It appears in the corner of the Maltese flag.

But the award didn't lift the siege, and by August 1942 the situation was critical. Only weeks of food remained. The Allies launched Operation Pedestal — the most heavily escorted convoy in history, 14 merchant ships shepherded by battleships, aircraft carriers, cruisers and destroyers through a gauntlet of Axis submarines and bombers. Of the 14 merchant ships that set out, only 5 made it through. Four warships were also sunk. But the convoy that limped into Grand Harbour on 15 August 1942 — the feast of Santa Marija — saved Malta. The crowds lining the harbour walls watched in silence as the ships came in, men removing their hats, women crossing themselves. The most celebrated survivor was the tanker *Ohio*, so badly damaged she had to be lashed between two destroyers to stay afloat, her back broken, her engines dead, but carrying 10,000 tons of fuel that Malta's aircraft needed to fly. She settled to the bottom of the harbour the moment her cargo was unloaded.

Within months, Malta's strategic position paid its dividend. Allied forces used it as the launchpad for the invasion of Sicily in July 1943, then mainland Italy in September, and the long road back through occupied Europe had its first real footing.

## The Island That Kept Saying Yes

Malta gained independence from Britain in 1964 and became a republic in 1974. It joined the European Union in 2004 and adopted the Euro in 2008. It is, today, one of the most densely populated countries in the world, a hub for financial services, gaming, maritime business, and a booming tourism industry.

And yet what strikes you, if you sit with Malta's history for any length of time, is how continuous the story is. This small limestone rock has been inhabited, without significant interruption, for over seven thousand years. Every wave of people who arrived — temple builders, Phoenicians, Carthaginians, Romans, Arabs, Normans, Knights, the British, the tourists — found something worth staying for. Something about the harbours, the light, the position at the hinge of the world, the limestone that carves easily and lasts forever.

Walk through Valletta and you're inside a Baroque city built by a military order of medieval knights on the foundations of a Roman town on the site of a Phoenician trading post, on an island where, a few miles away, there are temples that were old when civilisation was young. Sit at a café in the Upper Barrakka Gardens and look out over the Grand Harbour and understand, viscerally, why everyone always wanted this. The water is sapphire blue. The fortifications rise on every side. And somewhere under your feet, in the deep slow dark of the limestone, the Sleeping Lady is still curled in her ancient rest, undisturbed.
