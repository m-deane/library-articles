## FEATURE PROSE

In a dry dock at Cairnryan, Scotland, in the autumn of 1980, the last pieces of HMS Ark Royal were being cut apart for scrap. She had been the Royal Navy's last conventional aircraft carrier — the last ship capable of launching and recovering fixed-wing aircraft with catapults and arresting wires — and she was going into furnaces that would turn 43,000 tons of steel into rebar and structural sections for the construction industry. Former crew members travelled to the remote corner of Loch Ryan to stand at the breaker's jetty and watch. Some of them had served on her for years. Her sister ship, HMS Eagle, had arrived at Cairnryan three months earlier and was almost finished being dismantled. The two ships died within yards and weeks of each other, which was not planned but felt, to those who watched, entirely appropriate.

The scrapping of Ark Royal did not make headlines commensurate with what it represented. It was presented, when it was noted at all, as a routine end-of-life decommissioning — the same thing that happens to every ship eventually, the inevitable consequence of age and the economics of modern naval construction. What it actually was was the end of a capability that the Royal Navy had possessed, with interruptions, since 1918: the ability to put high-performance fixed-wing aircraft into the air from a ship at sea. After Ark Royal was scrapped, the Royal Navy had no aircraft carriers in the traditional sense for the better part of a decade — a gap without precedent in the history of any major naval power, a gap that would cost Britain dearly and nearly catastrophically in 1982, when the Argentine Air Force began attacking ships in the South Atlantic with aircraft that no British carrier existed to counter.

The story of how Britain got from the front of the queue — more carriers in 1939 than any other nation, the inventors of the carrier concept, the people who first thought of landing an aircraft on a ship — to a nation without carriers at all runs through the fire and water of the Second World War. To tell it properly, you have to name every ship.

---

Britain's carrier story begins with a converted Italian passenger liner. HMS Argus, formerly the *Conte Rosso*, was purchased by the Royal Navy in 1916 and rebuilt as a flush-deck aircraft carrier — the first fully operational carrier in history, the first ship from which aircraft could fly both on and off without modification. She entered service in 1918, weeks before the Armistice that made her combat use unnecessary, and she spent the interwar years as a training carrier, obsolete but useful, carrying aircraft to places that needed them and training the pilots who would fly the carriers that would matter.

The carriers that came after her were conversions and improvisations: HMS Furious, Courageous, and Glorious — all Courageous-class battlecruisers of the First World War, fast and lightly armoured, their enormous hulls ideal for conversion once the Washington Naval Treaty of 1922 required the Royal Navy to reduce its battleship numbers. Converted to carriers in the 1920s, they carried the Fleet Air Arm through the lean interwar years: Hermes (the first ship designed from the keel as a carrier, though she was small), the three conversions, and finally, in 1938, HMS Ark Royal — the first British carrier designed entirely with modern aviation requirements in mind, with two hangar decks, integral elevators, and a flight deck long enough for contemporary aircraft to land without being stopped by arresting gear alone.

In September 1939, the Royal Navy entered the war with seven carriers: Argus, Courageous, Furious, Glorious, Hermes, Eagle, and Ark Royal. By the end of 1942, five of them were gone.

---

HMS Courageous was the first to die. She was sunk on September 17, 1939, two weeks after the war began, by the German submarine U-29 while conducting anti-submarine patrols in the Western Approaches — the very role her surface-search aircraft were assigned to perform. The Admiralty had understood that using fleet carriers for U-boat hunting was operationally questionable, but the demand for air cover in the Atlantic had overwhelmed the available escort carriers, and Courageous was sent. U-29's commander, Kapitänleutnant Otto Schuhart, fired a spread of three torpedoes from a range of less than three miles. Two struck. Courageous capsized and sank in twenty minutes. Of her crew of 1,260, 519 were killed. The loss sent, in the words of the official Naval History, "a shudder through the Admiralty." Fleet carriers were immediately withdrawn from anti-submarine duty.

What replaced them in those waters were smaller ships that could do the job less glamorously: escort carriers converted from merchant hulls, HMS Audacity the first of them, crammed with Grumman Martlets and Swordfish torpedo planes. They were not Courageous. They were good enough for their purpose in a way that Courageous, used as an improvised tool for a mission she was never designed for, was not.

HMS Glorious was next, and her loss was different — more controversial, more debated, and in some ways more disturbing. She was sunk on June 8, 1940, during the evacuation of Allied forces from Norway, by the battlecruisers Scharnhorst and Gneisenau in what should have been navigable water on a route that had been used five times before without incident. She was caught with no aircraft airborne, her crow's nest unmanned, only twelve of her eighteen boilers lit — a combination of operational failures whose exact cause has been debated for eighty years, the Board of Inquiry sealed until 2041. Scharnhorst's first shot hit at a range of twenty-six kilometres — one of the longest-range hits in naval history, a shell that struck the forward flight deck and detonated in the upper hangar before Glorious could get a single aircraft into the air. She sank in two hours. Of approximately 1,560 officers and men aboard, 43 survived.

The two destroyers escorting her — HMS Ardent and HMS Acasta — fought back with the particular bravery of small ships against impossible odds, making smoke screens and firing torpedoes at vessels that outweighed them by a factor of twenty-five. Acasta's last torpedo run, made after the ship was already mortally hit, put a hole in Scharnhorst large enough to put her in dry dock for six months. Captain Charles Glasfurd of Acasta and Lieutenant Commander J.F. "Ben" Barker of Ardent were both recommended for posthumous Victoria Crosses for this action. Neither received one. The recommendation files disappeared into the same administrative closure that sealed the Board of Inquiry.

The son of Lieutenant Commander Barker, who decades later compiled the most detailed independent investigation of the sinking, noted that everything about Glorious was obscured — her reason for sailing independently, the signals that were sent and not received, the decision not to alert other ships in the area. "There has been a cover-up," he wrote, not as conspiracy theory but as documented fact. "The inquiry concluded the dead Captain was to blame. Had he survived, he would almost certainly have been court-martialled." The dead captain, by virtue of being dead, could not object.

---

HMS Ark Royal, the most famous British carrier of the war's first two years, was also the most improbable. German propaganda reported her sunk no fewer than seven times between 1939 and 1941, a propagandistic enthusiasm that became a running joke among her crew, who reportedly kept score of their own reported sinkings with something between dark amusement and professional pride. She hunted the Graf Spee in the South Atlantic, participated in the evacuation of Norway, launched the Swordfish torpedo strike that crippled the Bismarck's steering — the strike that allowed HMS Dorsetshire to finish the battleship with torpedoes and gave Britain one of its few unambiguous naval victories of 1941. She was good at her job and she had a crew that knew it.

She was sunk on November 13, 1941, by the German submarine U-81, east of Gibraltar. She had survived the seven propaganda deaths; she did not survive the real torpedo. The hit opened a flooding path that led through design flaws — covers left open during crew evacuation of lower decks, inadequate damage control initiated too late after the commanding officer gave the abandon ship order prematurely — and she was lost. Of her crew of 1,488, one man was killed. The near-miraculous preservation of her crew in a ship that sank more slowly than it should have was cold comfort to a Navy that now had, in the entire Mediterranean, only HMS Victorious and the damaged Illustrious. The subsequent Board of Inquiry court-martialled Captain Loben Maund for negligence. The lessons learned — primarily about damage control organisation and the necessity of maintaining watertight integrity during evacuation — were incorporated into the design modifications of subsequent Illustrious-class completions.

HMS Hermes died in the Indian Ocean on April 9, 1942, attacked by seventy Japanese aircraft from the fleet that had just raided Ceylon. She was hit forty times and sank with the loss of 307 of her 664 crew. She was the first carrier sunk by carrier-based aircraft in history — a distinction that would have struck her builders, who had designed her in 1917, as science fiction — and her death was partly a consequence of the same overextension that had killed Courageous three years earlier: a ship being used for purposes she was not designed for, in waters for which she was inadequate, because there was nothing else available.

◆

The building programme that attempted to replace these losses is itself a story about what a nation can do when the consequences of peace-time underinvestment come suddenly, completely, and simultaneously due.

The Illustrious class — HMS Illustrious, Formidable, Victorious, Indomitable, Implacable, and Indefatigable — were designed in the mid-1930s and built through the war years, the last two not entering service until 1944. They were better ships than what they replaced in almost every measurable way: better armoured (the 3-inch armoured hangar deck that proved its value when Illustrious survived five bomb hits in January 1941), better equipped, better supplied with the radar systems that had not existed when Ark Royal was designed. Their flaw was the one identified above: the armoured hangar's weight penalty reduced aircraft capacity to 36, which was not enough for the tasks they faced.

HMS Illustrious herself was the proof of concept for the armoured flight deck. Attacked by thirty-six Stuka dive-bombers off Malta in January 1941, she absorbed five bomb hits and two near misses and was still seaworthy. The battle damage officer's report noted that three of the five bombs detonated on the armoured deck and were contained; only two penetrated to the hangar below, starting fires that were controlled. An identical attack on a USS Ranger or USS Enterprise in 1941 — open-hangar American carriers — would, in the assessment of naval architects consulted by the Post-Mortem Board, have produced a catastrophic magazine detonation. Illustrious survived to fight in the Indian Ocean, the Pacific, and the bombardment of Japan's home islands. The trade-off was judged to have been correct.

What did all of this cost? The question is difficult to answer precisely because wartime accounting was not designed with retrospective analysis in mind, and because the exchange rates and price indices involved span eighty years. HMS Ark Royal (the 1937 ship) cost approximately £3 million to build in her pre-war form — equivalent, by standard Treasury GDP deflation, to roughly £240 million in 2024 terms. The Illustrious class cost between £2.5 million and £3.2 million each in wartime prices, depending on the state of materials costs and labour at time of construction — approximately £160–210 million each in 2024 terms. The light fleet carriers of the 1942 design, deliberately simplified for rapid construction, cost between £1.2 million and £1.7 million each.

Adding these figures across the full wartime construction programme — six Illustrious class, six light fleet carriers reaching completion by 1945, the earlier conversions of the 1930s — produces a total capital cost for British carrier aviation between 1935 and 1945 of something in the order of £25–30 million in wartime prices, or roughly £1.7–2 billion in 2024 terms. Against this must be set the losses: five carriers sunk between 1939 and 1942, their combined replacement cost at wartime prices approximately £12–15 million, or £800 million–£1 billion in 2024 terms. Britain bought two navies and kept one, at the end.

The man who perhaps understood this arithmetic most clearly was a 27-year-old sub-lieutenant named William "Bill" Stewart, who served on HMS Eagle — the 1924-vintage converted battleship Eagle, not the 1951 Audacious-class Eagle that came later — from 1939 to 1942. Stewart, from Dundee, had qualified as a pilot at RAF Cranwell and transferred to the Fleet Air Arm before the war, flying Swordfish torpedo planes off Eagle's deck in the early Mediterranean campaigns. He was aboard when Eagle was detailed to protect the Operation Pedestal convoy, and he was in the air — flying anti-submarine patrol north of the convoy — when U-73's four torpedoes struck his ship on August 11, 1942.

"I watched her go from about a thousand feet," he said in a 1987 interview with the Fleet Air Arm Museum's oral history project. "She took, I'd say, four minutes. Maybe five. The flight deck was level when the first torpedo hit. By the time I'd turned a full circle she was already showing keel."

Stewart ditched his Swordfish alongside a destroyer and was picked up. He went on to serve on HMS Formidable and ended the war as a lieutenant commander. He retired from the Fleet Air Arm in 1955 and became a schoolteacher in Fife. He died in 2002. At no point in his recorded recollections does he express particular bitterness about what happened — the loss of Eagle, the loss of colleagues, the improbable continuity of survival through four years of war. What he expressed, consistently and with a precision that characterised his accounts generally, was a specific frustration about the decision-making structures that had produced the situation he flew into.

"We had four carriers for that convoy," he told the oral historian in 1987. "The Admiralty thought four was sufficient. I can tell you from a Swordfish at a thousand feet, watching my ship go down in four minutes, that four was not sufficient. The mathematics required more. The mathematics always required more than we had."

---

The mathematics, after the war, produced a different kind of inadequacy. Britain emerged from the Second World War with more carriers than it had started with — the wartime construction programme had produced enough light fleet carriers to fill the gaps left by the losses — but the economic state of the country in 1945 precluded maintaining a two-ocean carrier navy. Carriers were expensive not just to build but to operate: each fleet carrier required a crew of 1,500–2,000 men, a dedicated air group of 36–80 aircraft (with their associated training, maintenance, and replacement costs), and a constant cycle of refit and modernisation that the straitened Admiralty budgets of the postwar period could not sustain.

The scrapping decisions began almost immediately after the war ended. Several of the 1942-design light fleet carriers — ships that had been built at enormous cost, that had not fired a shot in anger, that were seven or eight years from their designed service lives — were sold to other navies or paid off into reserve. Australia, Canada, India, France, Brazil, Argentina: the purchasers of Britain's surplus carriers read like the guest list for a postwar naval fire sale. By 1952, the Royal Navy's active carrier fleet had contracted to a fraction of its wartime peak.

What remained was HMS Eagle (the new one) and HMS Ark Royal (the new one) — the two Audacious-class carriers whose construction had been suspended during the war and resumed afterward. They were magnificent ships by any standard: 45,000 tons full load, 800-foot flight decks, capable of operating the jet aircraft that the wartime ships were not. Eagle was first, in 1951; Ark Royal followed in 1955, after a construction period of thirteen years that had given her hull time to corrode significantly before she ever put to sea. They were the last of the line.

The 1966 Defence White Paper effectively ended their story before they had reached middle age. Denis Healey's decision to cancel the planned replacement carrier (CVA-01, which would have been the largest ship Britain had ever built) and to phase out fixed-wing carrier aviation entirely by the mid-1970s was driven by Treasury calculations about what Britain could and could not afford to maintain. The carrier lobby within the Royal Navy fought the decision for years. The carriers themselves were retained longer than planned — Ark Royal was given a further £30 million refit in 1967 to extend her life — but the end was fixed. Ark Royal was decommissioned in 1979. Eagle had gone in 1978. Both were scrapped at Cairnryan.

From 1980 to the acceptance into service of the small STOVL carriers Invincible, Hermes (the second one, already old), and ultimately the Queen Elizabeth class, the Royal Navy operated without conventional carrier aviation for intervals that, taken together, represent the longest gap in carrier capability of any major naval power in the postwar era. The Falklands War of 1982 fought through that gap by the narrowest of margins — the Sea Harriers of HMS Hermes and HMS Invincible providing air cover that was sufficient, just, for the purpose — but the loss of HMS Sheffield to an Exocet missile, HMS Coventry and HMS Ardent and HMS Antelope to conventional bombs from aircraft that no carrier fighter was available to intercept, demonstrated with brutal precision what carrier aviation was for and what its absence cost.

---

◆

There is a room at the Fleet Air Arm Museum at RNAS Yeovilton in Somerset where the objects of several careers and several ships are displayed in glass cases: logbooks, wings, photographs, identification plates removed from aircraft before they went to the bottom of the sea. One of the cases contains items from HMS Glorious — a pilot's flying helmet, a squadron photograph, the cap badge of a naval officer who died in the Norwegian Sea in June 1940. The badge has been in a case since 1940. The inquiry into why it ended up in the sea is sealed until 2041.

Outside the museum, on the grass of the taxiway, a Swordfish biplane is parked in the open air. It is the same type of aircraft that flew from the decks of Glorious and Ark Royal and Eagle and Courageous — the fabric-covered, open-cockpit torpedo bomber that the Fleet Air Arm flew against battleships in 1941 — and it looks, in the twenty-first-century Somerset air, like an object from a different century, which it is. The museum keeps it airworthy. On occasional weekends, they fly it.

The Royal Navy has aircraft carriers again, finally, after the long gap: HMS Queen Elizabeth and HMS Prince of Wales, the two 65,000-ton vessels of the Queen Elizabeth class, commissioned in 2017 and 2019. They are the largest warships Britain has ever built. Their F-35B aircraft, which land vertically rather than on arresting wires, represent a different naval aviation philosophy than the ships scrapped at Cairnryan. Their construction was awarded in 2007, twenty-seven years after Ark Royal was cut apart. Their operational capability reached its initial state in 2020.

The gap between the last conventional carrier and the first modern one spans forty years, most of Britain's postwar defence reviews, one shooting war in the South Atlantic, the end of the Empire that the carriers were built to protect, and the deaths of most of the men who served on the ships that did not survive 1942. Bill Stewart, who watched Eagle go in four minutes from a Swordfish at a thousand feet, did not live to see the Queen Elizabeth class commission. He would have recognised the ships, and he might have said something precise and measured about the mathematics.

The Swordfish at Yeovilton is maintained in airworthy condition, which means it flies, occasionally, over Somerset. From the ground, on a clear day, you can watch it bank and turn — the same turn, the same wing loading, the same rattling persistence against the air — and it is impossible not to think of the men who banked and turned like this over Norwegian fjords and Sicilian channels and the cold grey water of the Norwegian Sea in 1940, and did not come back.

The aircraft is olive-drab and cream. It makes a sound like a very determined lawnmower. It is eighty years old and it still flies.

**SIDEBAR 1: EVERY BRITISH CARRIER — A SHIP-BY-SHIP ACCOUNTING, 1939–1945**

**HMS Argus** (1918) — Converted from the Italian *Conte Rosso*; 15,750 tons displacement; 20 aircraft; cost approximately £2m 1918 (conversion). Used as a training carrier throughout the war. Survived; sold for scrap 1946.

**HMS Furious** (1925 conversion) — Original Courageous-class battlecruiser; 22,450 tons; 36 aircraft; conversion cost approximately £0.8m. Used for Atlantic and Arctic convoy operations; participated in the attack on the Tirpitz in Norwegian fjords (Operation Tungsten, 1944). Survived; sold for scrap 1948.

**HMS Courageous** (1928 conversion) — Courageous class; 22,500 tons; 48 aircraft. **Sunk September 17, 1939** by U-29 in Western Approaches, anti-submarine patrol. Loss: 519 of 1,260 crew. Cost to replace: approximately £2.5 million (1939 prices).

**HMS Glorious** (1930 conversion) — Courageous class; 22,500 tons; 48 aircraft. **Sunk June 8, 1940** by Scharnhorst and Gneisenau, Norwegian Sea. Loss: approximately 1,519 of 1,560 crew. The worst single Royal Navy loss of the war. Board of Inquiry sealed until 2041.

**HMS Hermes** (1924) — First ship designed from keel as a carrier; 13,200 tons; 20 aircraft; cost £2m. **Sunk April 9, 1942** by Japanese carrier aircraft off Ceylon. Loss: 307 of 664 crew.

**HMS Ark Royal** (1938) — First modern British carrier; 27,000 tons; 60 aircraft; cost £3m (approximately £240m in 2024 terms). **Sunk November 14, 1941** by U-81, Mediterranean. Loss: 1 of 1,488 crew.

**HMS Eagle** (1924 conversion) — Converted from battleship Almirante Cochrane; 22,600 tons; 24 aircraft; cost approximately £1.5m (conversion). **Sunk August 11, 1942** by U-73, Mediterranean (Operation Pedestal). Loss: 260 of 1,160 crew.

**HMS Illustrious** (1940) — Illustrious class; 28,000 tons; 36 aircraft (armoured hangar); cost £2.8m. Hit five times by Stukas off Malta, January 1941; repaired in USA; survived. Sold for scrap 1956.

**HMS Formidable** (1941) — Illustrious class; 28,000 tons; 36 aircraft. Damaged in Mediterranean, 1941; survived. Sold for scrap 1953.

**HMS Victorious** (1941) — Illustrious class; 28,000 tons; 36 aircraft. Survived the entire war. Modernised 1950–1958; sold for scrap 1969.

**HMS Indomitable** (1941) — Modified Illustrious class; 30,000 tons; 45 aircraft. Damaged during Pedestal, August 1942; survived the war. Sold for scrap 1953.

**HMS Implacable & HMS Indefatigable** (1944) — Final Illustrious-class completions; 32,000 tons; 48 aircraft. Both sold for scrap by 1955.

Summary: Of 12 carriers operational at some point in WW2, 5 were sunk in combat (Courageous, Glorious, Hermes, Ark Royal, Eagle). The 5 losses represented approximately £13–15 million in 1939–1942 construction or replacement costs — roughly £900 million–£1.05 billion in 2024 terms.

**SIDEBAR 2: THE ARMISTICE SHIP AND THE DESIGN PROBLEM — WHY BRITAIN'S CARRIERS WERE ALWAYS BEHIND**

Britain invented the aircraft carrier. It is also true that Britain, for most of the Second World War, operated inferior carrier aircraft to its adversaries — a combination that is less paradoxical than it appears, and that tells something important about what happened to British aviation between the wars.

The problem was institutional: the Fleet Air Arm (FAA) was controlled by the Royal Air Force from 1918 to 1939. The RAF, focused on strategic bombing and home defence, had minimal interest in developing specialised naval aviation — low, slow torpedo bombers, dive-bombers capable of ship attack, fighters with long range and carrier compatibility. The result was that in 1939, British carrier aircraft were generally inferior in performance to the aircraft of both major adversaries: the Fleet Air Arm flew the Fairey Swordfish (biplane torpedo bomber, maximum speed 138 mph), the Blackburn Roc (turret fighter, maximum speed 196 mph), and the Fairey Fulmar (two-seat fleet fighter, maximum speed 280 mph). The Japanese Zero had a maximum speed of 332 mph. The German Me 109F reached 373 mph.

The return of the FAA to Royal Navy control in May 1939 did not immediately solve the problem — it takes years to design, develop, and manufacture competitive aircraft — but it did initiate the procurement of American aircraft (the Grumman Martlet/Wildcat and later the Corsair and Hellcat) that eventually gave British carriers a competitive air group. The irony that Britain's most effective carrier aircraft of the war — the Avenger, the Corsair, the Hellcat — were American-designed is not trivial: it reflects twenty years of institutional neglect that could not be corrected by institutional reform alone.

**SIDEBAR 3: THE FALKLANDS GAP — WHAT HAPPENED WHEN THE CARRIERS WERE GONE**

On May 4, 1982, HMS Sheffield was struck by an AM.39 Exocet missile fired by an Argentine Navy Super Étendard aircraft. Sheffield was a Type 42 destroyer, a modern warship; the Exocet was a French-designed anti-ship missile that the Argentine Navy had purchased three years earlier. Sheffield was struck because there was no carrier-based fighter aircraft available to intercept the Super Étendard before it reached its launch point.

There were British carriers in the South Atlantic: HMS Hermes and HMS Invincible. Both carried Sea Harrier fighters — STOVL aircraft that landed vertically and did not require catapults. The Sea Harriers performed remarkably, destroying 21 Argentine aircraft in air-to-air combat against zero losses to enemy fighters. But they could not maintain a continuous combat air patrol over the entire task force area simultaneously; the gap in coverage was where Sheffield was attacked.

Four British warships were sunk by Argentine aircraft during the Falklands War: HMS Sheffield (Exocet), HMS Coventry (bombs), HMS Ardent (bombs), HMS Antelope (bombs). An additional ship, the Atlantic Conveyor, carrying heavy-lift helicopters, was sunk by Exocet. The losses were survivable, militarily — Britain won the war — but they were a precise, empirical demonstration of the cost of operating without conventional fixed-wing carrier aviation. Naval architects and defence analysts who reviewed the campaign identified that a single fleet carrier with a conventional air group would likely have prevented all four warship losses and the loss of Atlantic Conveyor. The replacement cost of those five ships exceeded £400 million in 1982 prices — more than twice the cost of the CVA-01 carrier that Denis Healey cancelled in 1966.
