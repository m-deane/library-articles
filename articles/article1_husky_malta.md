## FEATURE PROSE

The limestone of Valletta is the colour of old bone. It has been this colour since the Knights of St. John quarried it from the island's bedrock in the sixteenth century, and it was this colour on the morning of June 11, 1940, when twenty-five Savoia-Marchetti SM.79 bombers came in from Sicily and began the Siege of Malta. The bombs fell on a city that had not yet built public shelters. They fell on the Grand Harbour and the Three Cities and the streets of the Cospicua district, and the limestone dust they raised drifted back down over everything, covering the dead and the living alike in a fine, pale powder that was, in a way, the island itself — ground up and redistributed by people who wanted it to stop existing.

Three years later, on the evening of July 9, 1943, a Maltese dockworker named Salvu Farrugia stood on the Barrakka bastions above that same harbour and watched something he would spend the rest of his life trying to describe. Every ship in the Allied fleet — 600 vessels, everything from battleships to landing craft to converted ferries loaded with Canadian infantry — was moving at once. They were moving north, toward Sicily, ninety kilometres away across the water. The lights were blacked out and the engines were low, but the sheer displacement of that many hulls in that small a body of water created a vibration that Farrugia later recalled not as sound but as a feeling in the soles of his feet, transmitted up through the limestone terrace where he stood, as if the island itself were humming.

"I thought of my mother's house in Senglea," he said decades later, according to testimony collected by Malta's National War Museum. "The Germans had taken the roof off it. I thought, maybe now we will get it back."

---

What happened to Malta between those two moments — between the first bombers and the departing fleet — is one of the most extraordinary civilian stories of the Second World War, and one of the least understood outside the island itself. The scale of it is almost impossible to comprehend without standing in Valletta and looking at the evidence still embedded in the limestone walls: the pockmarks from cannon fragments, the replaced sections of masonry in shades slightly lighter than the surrounding stone, the rebuilt dome of the Carmelite Church, destroyed in 1942 and reconstructed so perfectly that you would not know it had ever fallen, unless someone told you, which the Maltese always do.

The numbers, stated baldly, are inadequate to the experience they represent. In April 1942 alone — the worst single month — 6,500 tons of bombs fell on Malta, an island one-fifth the size of Greater London. In the same period the entire Blitz deposited 18,000 tons on London over eight months. In the first half of 1942, from January 1 to July 24, there was exactly one twenty-four-hour period when no bomb fell anywhere on the island. One. The rest was continuous. Malta's population of 270,000 — a number that does not include the British garrison — spent those months underground in limestone caves and specially cut shelters, living on a ration that by the spring of 1942 had dropped to below subsistence level. By May of that year, Joseph Galea Debono, who would later become President of the George Cross Island Association, recalls the situation in terms stripped of rhetoric: "No munitions. No fuel. No food, basically, no medicines. The island was on the point of surrendering."

That it did not surrender is a fact. Why it did not surrender is a more complicated story, one that lives in the space between official history and personal memory — in the words of the woman who kept her family alive on broadcast flour and boiled carob pods, in the hands of the Maltese stonemasons who cut new shelters into the rock at night while German Me-109s circled overhead, in the quiet obstinacy of a culture that had survived the Great Siege of 1565 and the depredations of the Knights and the famines of the nineteenth century, and that experienced each new catastrophe not as an aberration but as the continuation of a very long story in which Malta had, so far, always survived.

On April 15, 1942, King George VI awarded the George Cross — Britain's highest civilian decoration, second only to the Victoria Cross — to the island of Malta. His handwritten letter to the Governor read: *"To honour her brave people, I award the George Cross to the Island Fortress of Malta, to bear witness to a heroism and devotion that will long be famous in history."* It was, at that point, the only time the award had ever been given to a place rather than a person. The ceremony to present the actual medal was delayed for months because the bombing was too intense to gather people safely in a public square. The island received its highest honour under conditions that precluded a public celebration.

Carmen Vella was eleven years old when the siege began, the eldest child of a baker in the village of Mosta, fifteen kilometres from Valletta. Her father, Lorenzo, had converted his bakehouse into a shelter when it became clear in 1941 that the Germans were not going to stop. He had removed the bread ovens — which he could no longer fire anyway, the flour being insufficient — and replaced them with straw mattresses and a single oil lamp. Carmen's job, every morning before school, was to climb to the roof of their building and assess the bomb damage in the surrounding streets, then report which routes were passable to her mother.

"I was a spotter," she told historian Nicholas Vella in a 2003 oral history project commissioned by the Maltese government. "Not an official spotter, just — someone had to know which roads had craters."

By the spring of 1942, Carmen had missed enough school that the distinction between school days and non-school days had become academic. The classroom in Mosta had been requisitioned for use as a supply depot. Her younger brother, Toni, had developed rickets — the curved leg bones of severe vitamin D deficiency — which Carmen could see developing week by week as the rations contracted. Typhoid had reached epidemic proportions. A neighbour's child had died of it. The German propaganda radio, which Carmen could hear on her father's wireless set, reported repeatedly that Malta had been neutralised, which she found baffling since the bombs were still falling.

What kept the Vella family going — what kept most Maltese families going, according to the oral histories — was not ideology or patriotism in any formal sense, but something more granular and practical: the shared project of daily survival, which on Malta had a centuries-long precedent and a highly developed local technology. The limestone shelters. The communal food distribution at the Victory Kitchens, which the British government established in 1942 to prevent the total collapse of civilian nutrition. The informal networks of information — who had a little extra bread, which harbour was still operational enough to receive a submarine bringing quinine from Gibraltar. The knowledge, passed through these networks with the reliability of deep cultural memory, that this had happened before, and that Malta was still here.

"My grandmother told me about the plague years," Carmen Vella said in 2003. "Her grandmother had told her. We were not new to this."

---

In the tunnelled rooms below the Lascaris Battery in Valletta — seventeen metres of solid rock between the operations floor and the sky — a different kind of survival was being planned. The Lascaris War Rooms had served throughout the siege as the British headquarters for Malta's defence, its walls hung with plotting tables and communications equipment, its air thick with the smell of cigarette smoke and damp limestone. By early 1943, the mission had changed. The rooms had been expanded and upgraded. New communication lines had been run through the rock. And on a day in the spring of 1943, General Dwight D. Eisenhower, Admiral Andrew Cunningham, General Bernard Montgomery, and Air Marshal Arthur Tedder descended together into the cool underground, spread their maps across the plotting tables, and began to finalise the plans for what would become Operation Husky — the invasion of Sicily, and the opening move of the Allied campaign to retake Europe from its southern underbelly.

Air Vice-Marshal Sir Keith Park, commanding Malta's air forces, had known for months that the island's role was changing. Park, 50, was a New Zealander who had commanded 11 Group RAF during the Battle of Britain and who had arrived in Malta in July 1942 with a mandate not just to defend the island but to transform its air forces from a reactive defensive posture into an offensive one. He was a lean, angular man who habitually toured his airfields by personal Hurricane, landing unannounced at RAF Luqa or RAF Ta' Qali or RAF Ħal Far to speak directly with ground crew and pilots, a habit that made him both loved and feared. By late 1942, after the arrival of enough Spitfires to break the back of the Axis air campaign, Park had done what he was sent to do: Malta's aircraft were attacking Axis shipping again, and the island was, as Churchill had named it, earning its designation as an "unsinkable aircraft carrier" in the middle of the Mediterranean.

For Husky, Park's role would be air cover — both of the assembly areas in Malta's harbours and airfields, and of the invasion fleet itself during its ninety-kilometre crossing to Sicily. The mathematics of what this required were staggering. Every airfield on Malta, including the newly constructed Ta' Lambert strip on Gozo, was packed to operational capacity. Underground fuel storage — new tanks cut into the rock at the direct order of Lord Gort, Malta's governor — had been increased fivefold, from 3,500 to 18,000 tons. Every hour of July 1943 that was not devoted to attacking the Axis was devoted to preparing for the night of the 9th.

The 1st Canadian Infantry Division arrived in Malta in stages, some from the United Kingdom and some from Tunisia, their landing craft threading through a harbour so crowded with Allied shipping that navigation was nearly impossible. Major-General Guy Simonds, 40, commanded them — the youngest divisional commander in the Canadian Army, a Gunner by training who had never commanded an infantry division in battle. This would be Canada's first large-scale amphibious operation, the first time the country's soldiers had stormed an enemy shore since the catastrophe at Dieppe in August 1942, where 3,369 of 6,086 Canadian soldiers who went ashore were killed, wounded, or captured in less than nine hours.

The memory of Dieppe sat over every Canadian NCO and private in Malta's staging camps like a second atmosphere, spoken of obliquely, absorbed into the way men cleaned their rifles and folded their kit. Private William McLeod, of the Royal Canadian Regiment, wrote in a letter to his mother in Truro, Nova Scotia, that "the boats we practised on are bigger than the ones at Dieppe were." It was not exactly reassuring, but it was what he had.

Simonds had been briefed at the Lascaris War Rooms and had come away with two things fixed in his mind. The first was the absolute necessity of taking the Pachino airfield quickly — without it, the Allied air umbrella over the beachhead would be compromised from the start. The second was a private conversation he had had with a Maltese liaison officer, a man named Lieutenant Dr. Joseph Micallef, who had served throughout the siege as both a medical officer and, informally, as a source of local knowledge for the British command. Micallef had told Simonds something that the maps and the intelligence briefings had not: that the Sicilian coastline they were landing on was known to Maltese fishermen, that the channels and shallows and rocks around the Pachino peninsula had been navigated by men from Malta for five hundred years, and that if Simonds wanted pilots for the landing craft who would not run aground in the dark, he should ask for Maltese ones.

Simonds did ask. Several of the landing craft that carried the 1st Canadian Division to Sicily that night were piloted by Maltese fishermen. This detail does not appear in the official Canadian military history. It appears in Micallef's own memoir, published in Malta in 1958.

---

The weather on the night of July 9 was wrong. A Force 5 northwest gale — unexpected, late-season — had turned the Sicilian Channel into a churning grey-green chaos of steep, short waves that swamped landing craft, terrified paratroopers, and very nearly caused Eisenhower to postpone the entire operation. The Allied command at Lascaris watched weather reports through the afternoon with a controlled desperation that Park later described as "the worst few hours I spent in that room, and I had spent some very bad hours there."

The paradox — one of history's more perverse gifts — was that the same storm that was killing Allied paratroopers in wrong drop zones and drowning soldiers in capsized landing craft was also convincing the Italian and German defenders on the Sicilian coast that no invasion was possible. General Alfredo Guzzoni, commanding the Italian Sixth Army on the island, had reduced his forces to low alert. When the 150,000-man Allied force began coming ashore in the early hours of July 10, 1943, they met defenders who were, in several sectors, genuinely surprised.

The 231st Infantry Brigade — the Malta Brigade, recruited from the island's garrison — landed at what the operational planners called Bark East, on the southeast corner of Sicily, and began moving inland. They were Maltese and British soldiers who had spent years defending a tiny island, who had watched their city being bombed and their food supply dwindle to almost nothing, and who were now, for the first time, moving in the other direction. Toward the enemy rather than away from him. The symbolism was not lost on the men. Sergeant Major George Calleja, a Maltese NCO who had been with the garrison since 1940, wrote in a letter home: "We are in Sicily. I cannot quite believe it."

Carmen Vella heard the armada leave from the roof of her father's house in Mosta. She had been woken at midnight by the sound — not engines, because the engines were throttled back, but the vibration of displacement, the deep bass movement of hundreds of hulls clearing Grand Harbour. She climbed to the roof in her nightdress, as she had climbed it every morning for three years to look for bomb craters, and she stood there in the dark listening to a sound she had no name for and tried to understand what she was feeling.

"It was not happiness," she said in 2003, choosing her words with care. "It was something else. Like when the monsoon breaks after a long dry season — relief, but also something bigger than relief. The sense that the world had turned."

In Sicily, thirteen hours later, her father's cousin — a man she had never met, a fisherman from Marsaxlokk named Emmanuel Farrugia who had piloted a Maltese luzzu through the minefields off the Pachino coast in the dark — was guiding a landing craft carrying twenty Canadian soldiers toward a beach he had last visited before the war. The beach, on Sicilian soil, was three degrees of latitude north of where he had been born, and as the keel scraped sand and the ramp went down, he cut the engine, sat in the empty craft, and did not immediately move.

Later he would say he was not sure what he was thinking. Perhaps nothing, in the way that intense moments sometimes empty the mind. But beside the tiller, under the gunwale where he had stowed it carefully when he embarked, was a small wooden statue of the Madonna of Porto Salvo — Our Lady of Safe Arrival — that fishermen from Marsaxlokk had carried for three hundred years on every voyage into uncertain water. He had brought it with him on the greatest uncertain voyage of his life. He sat with it for a moment in the empty boat, while Canada's soldiers ran up a Sicilian beach, and then he turned the craft around and went back for the next load.

---

Operation Husky ran for thirty-eight days. By August 17, 1943, when the last German forces withdrew across the Strait of Messina — evacuating, in an operation the Allies failed to prevent, more than 100,000 soldiers who would prolong the Italian campaign for two more years — Sicily was Allied territory. Mussolini had fallen from power on July 25, arrested by his own King after the Fascist Grand Council voted against him. The Italian government that replaced him was already negotiating surrender. The Mediterranean, for the first time since 1941, was open to Allied shipping.

For Malta, the victory had an additional, more personal dimension. In August 1943, King George VI visited the island — the first British monarch to visit Malta in wartime, the first to set foot there since 1912. He came by ship, a destroyer running into Grand Harbour in the early morning, and the harbour walls were lined with Maltese people, tens of thousands of them, standing on the same limestone bastions where three years of bombing had driven them underground. They had come out, finally, fully, into the open air.

Winston Churchill came too, that same month, and stood in the ruins of the Auberge d'Auvergne and looked at what the Luftwaffe had done to Valletta's baroque architecture — the roofless palaces, the fallen domes, the facades standing without buildings behind them, the streets where medieval stone walls had been reduced to rubble — and he said, according to the account of Lord Mountbatten who was present, nothing at all. He stood looking for a long time without speaking.

---

◆

Carmen Vella's brother Toni recovered from rickets once the rationing ended. His legs straightened over several years, though they never entirely lost the slight curve that the photographs from the worst period show, the photographs their mother kept in a tin box and which Carmen kept after her mother died, and which her daughter keeps now, in a flat in Sliema overlooking the same water where the armada sailed. Toni became a civil engineer. He worked on the reconstruction of the Carmelite Church dome. He died in 1998.

The Lascaris War Rooms are now a museum. You descend the same stairs Eisenhower descended, into the same cool limestone air, and you find the plotting tables exactly as they were — the call signs, the sector maps, the telephone headsets on their cradles — and the guides will tell you, accurately, that the invasion of Europe was planned here. In a room seventeen metres underground on an island that in 1942 had no roof tiles left intact, that had been officially described by German High Command as having "nothing left to bomb."

The George Cross awarded to Malta in 1942 is on display in the War Museum in Fort St. Elmo, at the tip of Valletta's promontory, in a glass case beside the letter from King George VI. The cross itself is silver, small — smaller than you expect, somehow, for the weight it has been asked to carry. Outside the museum, on the limestone flagstones of the courtyard, you can see the star-shaped scars where cannon fragments hit the stone and could not be repaired, only smoothed over. The stone absorbed them. The stone absorbed everything. That is what stone does.

Grand Harbour is a working harbour still, container ships and ferries threading past the battlements where Salvu Farrugia stood the night the fleet sailed north. The water is clear and very blue, the limestone pale as old bone in the afternoon light, and if you stand long enough on the Barrakka bastions in the right light at the right angle you can almost hear, or perhaps you imagine you can hear, the deep vibration of a thousand engines moving very slowly in the dark, turning north, toward Sicily, toward the continent, toward the end of the longest siege.

## SIDEBARS

**SIDEBAR 1: THE GLADIATORS — FAITH, HOPE, AND CHARITY**

When Italy declared war on June 10, 1940, Malta's entire air defence consisted of four Gloster Gladiator biplane fighters — relics of a pre-war era already obsolete when the first Savoia-Marchetti bombers appeared over the island the following morning. Three of the four were airworthy. In the absence of official names, Maltese civilians — in a piece of spontaneous mythology — named the three aircraft Faith, Hope, and Charity.

The names were never officially sanctioned by the RAF and may have originated in a newspaper report, but they acquired an independent life the moment they appeared in print. The three biplanes, slower than every Italian aircraft they faced and outnumbered by margins of between 25 and 50 to one in early raids, became the symbol of the siege's opening chapter. Charity was damaged beyond repair in late 1940. Hope was destroyed in early 1941. Faith survived — she is preserved today in the Malta Aviation Museum at Ta' Qali — and became the physical embodiment of the siege's improbable continuity, the one aircraft from that first impossible summer that endured long enough to see the armada in the harbour.

The George Cross Island Association notes that Faith flew 112 operational sorties and was hit in combat multiple times without being shot down. She was retired from service in 1942 and her logbook, like the cross itself, is on display at the National War Museum. There is a particular pleasure in the fact that the aircraft named Faith outlasted Hope and Charity. It was, under the circumstances, theologically appropriate.

---

**SIDEBAR 2: THE LASCARIS WAR ROOMS — A SHELTER THAT CHANGED DIRECTION**

The tunnel complex beneath the Lascaris Battery was not originally designed as a command centre. The Lascaris tunnels were cut in the seventeenth century by the Knights of St. John as a series of communication passages and storage vaults connecting the upper and lower sections of Valletta's defences. The British military inherited them, extended them, and electrified them in the early twentieth century. By 1939 they were in use as a signals facility.

The transformation into a full operational headquarters began in 1940, when the scale of the German and Italian air campaign made it clear that any above-ground command facility would be destroyed within weeks. Work proceeded at night and on shifts, Maltese labourers cutting new rooms and new connecting passages, installing ventilation systems and communication cables, laying the linoleum floors and installing the illuminated plotting tables that the radar and observer networks would use to track incoming aircraft.

At its peak the War Rooms employed more than 400 personnel — British officers, Maltese civilian staff, Royal Navy signalers, women of the Women's Royal Naval Service who worked as plotters and telephone operators — running twenty-four-hour shifts in a space that was always cool, always slightly damp, always lit by artificial light, and always, thanks to the seventeen metres of limestone overhead, completely safe from bombing. The work done in those rooms — tracking, plotting, directing interception, managing convoys — is credited by historians with making the difference between Malta's survival and its surrender during the worst months of 1942.

The rooms were restored and opened to the public in 1972. The Eisenhower chair — the actual chair, according to museum records — is still there.

---

**SIDEBAR 3: OPERATION MINCEMEAT AND THE MAN WHO NEVER WAS**

The invasion of Sicily was so obviously the next Allied move after North Africa that Eisenhower's intelligence staff assessed in early 1943 that any deception operation would require something genuinely audacious to have any chance of diverting German attention. What they came up with was among the stranger operations of the entire war.

Naval intelligence officer Ewen Montagu and RAF Squadron Leader Charles Cholmondeley proposed using a human corpse — dressed as a Royal Marine officer, equipped with carefully forged identity documents and a briefcase containing letters purporting to reveal that the real Allied invasion was aimed at Greece and Sardinia, not Sicily — and allowing it to wash ashore in neutral Spain, where German intelligence contacts would find it and forward the documents to Berlin.

The corpse used was that of Glyndwr Michael, a 34-year-old homeless man from Wales who had died of rat poison in a London warehouse. He became Major William Martin, Royal Marines, with a fiancée named Pam (the letters were real, supplied by a woman who agreed to the use of her correspondence), unpaid bills from his London tailor, and a new name.

German intelligence received the documents. Hitler personally ordered reinforcements sent to Greece. Defensive installations in the Peloponnese were doubled. The German general in charge of the Balkans theatre was told to expect the main assault. When the first Allied soldiers waded ashore in Sicily on July 10, two of Germany's best divisions were waiting in the wrong country, 1,500 kilometres to the east.
